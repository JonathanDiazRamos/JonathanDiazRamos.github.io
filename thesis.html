Thesis

AI-Based Environmental Evaluation

A Machine Learning Approach to Recognize Environmental Features Associated with Social Factors

In this thesis, we seek to enhance the Climate and Economic Justice Screening Tool (CEJST) by introducing five additional environmental indicators, derived from Google Street View (GSV) images, to evaluate census tracts. We developed models capable of detecting garbage bags, greenery, and three types of road damage (longitudinal, transverse, and alligator cracks) through image classification, object detection, and segmentation techniques. These models were applied to 110 census tracts across Phoenix, Chicago, and Atlanta, enabling us to gather granular environmental data. Our results reveal that using GSV images in this context can provide valuable insights for federal agencies considering environmental and socioeconomic factors in disadvantaged communities.

The advantages of GSV as a data source are clear: it is accessible, cost-effective, and offers a level of granularity that surpasses current census tract evaluation methods. GSV images cost only $7 per 1,000 images, allowing us to efficiently monitor changes and patterns in specific environmental conditions over time. Given Google’s extensive collection of monthly 360° images across many locations, we have access to a vast dataset that spans over a decade. This makes GSV a promising, sustainable resource for evaluating urban environments, offering a timely and economical way to assess various census tracts in greater detail and to track the effectiveness of federal interventions.

Our analysis, however, underscores the importance of selecting features that reflect the unique characteristics of each city. For example, Phoenix’s high temperatures impact greenery throughout the year, regardless of census tract classification, which limits its value as a distinguishing feature. This insight suggests that feature spaces should be adjusted according to specific urban and environmental factors to optimize the accuracy of census tract assessments. Ultimately, by refining our understanding of which features work best in different regions, we can create a robust, adaptable methodology for supplementing the CEJST. This approach offers federal agencies a reliable tool to support climate and economic justice efforts across diverse urban landscapes.
